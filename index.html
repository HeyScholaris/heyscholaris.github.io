Section 1

What This Game Is

A familiar d20 chassis built for fast, cinematic sci-fantasy play.

This game is about

    dangerous jobs in a fractured galaxy

    small crews making a big difference

    blasters, starships, and relic blades

    rebellion cells and bounty contracts

    cyberware, syndicates, and black markets

    rare, dangerous users of the Force

This ruleset uses

    d20 + modifiers for almost everything

    Advantage / Disadvantage for simple bonuses and penalties

    Edge and Threat to make rolls cinematic

    Callings and Paths for character identity

    Talents for customization

    Heat and Reputation to drive campaigns

Section 2

Core Rules

The basic roll, advantage, cinematic momentum, and contested actions.

The Basic Roll

When an action matters, roll 1d20 + Attribute + Skill. If the total meets or beats the target number, you succeed. You roll against either a Difficulty or an enemy's Defense.

DifficultyBenchmark
10Easy
12Standard
15Hard
18Extreme
22Legendary

Advantage and Disadvantage

    Advantage: roll 2d20 and keep the higher.

    Disadvantage: roll 2d20 and keep the lower.

    If both apply, they cancel each other out.

Edge

Gain Edge when you succeed by 5 or more, or when you roll a natural 20.

Spend 1 Edge to deal +1d6 damage, move an extra short distance, disarm/shove/trip/pin a target, gain or improve cover, help an ally gain Advantage, reduce task time, avoid collateral damage, spot a useful detail, or keep a weapon from overheating or running dry.

Threat

The GM gains Threat when you fail by 5 or more, or when you roll a natural 1.

Use 1 Threat to introduce reinforcements, alarms, target repositioning, weapon failure, lockdowns, social suspicion, ship strain, or civilian danger. Threat should intensify scenes, not shut them down.

Contested Rolls

If two characters directly oppose each other, both roll 1d20 + relevant Attribute + relevant Skill. The higher result wins.

Hiding vs Notice Deceive vs Discipline Command vs Discipline Force vs Discipline Pilot vs Pilot

Character Creation

Create a character in six steps.

Step 1 — Assign Attributes

Assign these modifiers across the six Attributes:

+2+2+1+10−1
Step 2 — Choose a Background

A Background tells the group who you were before the campaign. It grants 2 trained skills, 1 contact, and 1 useful item or perk.

Step 3 — Choose a Calling

Your Calling is your main role. At level 1 it gives trained skills, starting features, and starting gear suggestions.

Step 4 — Choose Trained Skills

You begin with 2 trained skills from Background and 4 trained skills from Calling. If a skill would be gained twice, choose another from the same list.

Step 5 — Choose Starting Equipment

Take one primary weapon, one backup weapon or tool, one armor set, a field kit tied to your role, and one signature item from your background.

Step 6 — Record Derived Stats

Section 5

Combat Rules

Fast rounds, simple movement, and cinematic tactical choices.

Each Round1 Action
Each Round1 Move
Each Round1 Reaction

Movement

    across a small room

    between pieces of cover

    up a corridor segment

    into melee with a nearby target

    If using a grid, Move is 6 squares.

Common Actions

Attack Dash Aim Hide Assist Use Item Hack Stabilize Use the Force Ready
Attack Roll: roll 1d20 + relevant Attribute + combat Skill and compare the result to the target's Defense. Melee uses Body or Agility + Melee. Ranged uses Agility + Ranged.

Damage and Critical Hits

    On a hit, roll the weapon's damage.

    On a natural 20, deal maximum normal damage, then roll the weapon damage dice again and add the result.

    Optional critical effects: disarm target, knock target prone, force target into cover, destroy carried gear, or inflict 1 extra Wound on a Downed target.

Cover

    Half cover: +2 Defense

    Full cover: +5 Defense

    Targets behind full cover usually cannot be hit unless the attack can arc, explode, pierce, or ignore cover.

Range BandMeaning
Engagedclose enough for melee
Shortsame room or close lane
Mediumacross a street, hangar, or long hall
Longrooftop to rooftop, sniper line, open field
Extremespecialized weapons or ship scale

Aiming, Hiding, and Helping

    Aim: If you spend your Action aiming, your next ranged attack before the end of your next turn has Advantage.

    Hide: Roll Stealth against enemy Notice.

    Assist: As an Action, help an ally. If the GM agrees the help matters, that ally gains Advantage on one related roll.

Grappling

To grapple, roll Athletics vs the target's Athletics or Agility. On success, the target is Restrained until they break free, you move away, you are forced off, or you release them.

    Restrained targets cannot move freely.

    They have Disadvantage on Agility-based actions.

    They are easier to capture.

Downed, Wounds, and Death

    At 0 Health, you are Downed.

    At the start of each of your turns, roll 1d20.

    10+: you hold on.

    9 or less: mark 1 Wound.

    At 3 Wounds, you die.

Healing

    An ally can stabilize you with Medicine 12, a medkit and a successful Medicine check, or a feature or Force power that restores Health.

    Once stabilized, you remain unconscious until healed.

    Medkits restore Health outside combat and may stabilize during combat.

    Force healing restores Health directly.

    A Full Rest restores all Health unless the GM rules a severe injury needs care.

Section 6

Force Rules

Resolve, Force powers, strain, and optional moral pressure.

Who Can Use the Force

Only characters with Force training may take the Force skill and use Force powers. In the core rules, that is normally the Adept Calling, though the GM may allow rare training or special talents for others.

Resolve

Resolve measures focus, spiritual control, and inner reserves. You spend Resolve to use Force powers and some special Calling features.

Starting Resolve3 + Will
Using a Force Power

Choose the power, spend the listed Resolve cost (usually 1), then roll 1d20 + Will + Force and compare to a target's Defense or a set Difficulty.

Force Strain

If you use a Force power while at 0 Resolve, you may still use it, but you take 1 Strain.

    Each point of Strain gives −1 to Force rolls.

    Strain remains until a Full Rest.

    At 3 Strain, the GM may trigger a backlash.

Possible Backlash

    a visible aura

    emotional overflow

    objects shaking or breaking

    attracting hostile attention

    local electronics glitching

    revealing your location through the Force

Force Powers
Push / Pull — Cost 1 Resolve: Move a nearby creature or object a short distance. Attack use: roll vs Defense. On a hit, deal 1d6 force damage and push the target.
Heighten — Cost 1 Resolve: Gain Advantage on one Initiative, Notice, Agility, or Athletics roll this scene.
Sense — Cost 1 Resolve: Ask one question the Force might answer, such as whether danger is near, life is nearby, hostile intent is being hidden, or the Force was used here recently.
Leap — Cost 1 Resolve: Make an impossible jump or fall safely this turn.
Bind — Cost 1 Resolve: Roll Force vs target Defense or Discipline. On a success, the target is restrained until the end of your next turn.
Deflect — Cost 1 Resolve, Reaction: Reduce incoming ranged damage by 1d8 + Will. If damage is reduced to 0, you may redirect the shot at a valid target within Short range.
Mind Trick — Cost 1 Resolve: Roll Force vs target Discipline. On success, plant a short, plausible suggestion. The request cannot obviously force self-destruction unless the GM allows a powerful narrative moment.
Surge — Cost 1 Resolve: Deal 1d8 energy damage to a nearby target, or add +1d8 to a melee attack this turn.
Heal — Cost 1 Resolve: Restore 1d8 + Will Health to a touched target. Each target can benefit from Heal once per Short Rest unless a feature says otherwise.
Cloak — Cost 1 Resolve: Gain Advantage on Stealth and become difficult to track until you attack, shout, or take obvious hostile action.
Force and Morality: This game does not require a fixed morality chart, but cruel, reckless, or fear-fueled Force use should feel dangerous. Optional rule: when a Force user acts through cruelty, domination, or rage, they gain 1 extra Strain after the power resolves.

Section 7

Cyberware Rules

Powerful implants with social and spiritual tradeoffs.

Cyber Load Limit2 + Tech
Over Limit Penalty−1 Presence
Over Limit Penalty−1 Discipline

Cyber Load

Each implant has a Load value. If your total Load exceeds your safe Cyber Load, for every point over the limit you suffer:

    −1 Presence

    −1 Discipline

The GM may also rule that some NPCs distrust heavily altered characters on sight.

Installing Cyberware

Installing cyberware requires access to the implant, proper tools, a qualified installer or facility, and time and money or barter. The GM may turn installation into downtime, a mission reward, or a black-market scene.

Cyberware and the Force: The Force still works through cybernetic bodies, but severe overload can disrupt emotional control. If a Force user is over their Cyber Load limit, they also take −1 Force for every 2 points over the limit, rounded down.

Section 9

Talents

Talents may be taken whenever your level grants one, or when the GM offers one as a special reward.

Combat Talents

    Deadeye: Ignore Half Cover with ranged attacks.

    Duelist: Gain +1 Defense while wielding a one-handed melee weapon with your off-hand empty.

    Two-Gun Style: When attacking with a Light pistol, make one off-hand shot at -2.

    Executioner: Deal +2 damage to wounded targets.

    Quick Draw: Draw or ready a weapon for free. Gain Advantage on Initiative if combat starts suddenly.

    Suppressive Fire: When using Autofire, targets struck suffer -2 on their next attack unless they take cover.

    Close Quarters: No penalty for firing ranged weapons while engaged in melee.

    Heavy Hitter: Once per turn, reroll one melee damage die.

    Breacher: Ignore 1 point of armor when using shotguns, heavy melee weapons, or breaching tools.

    Pinning Shot: When you spend Edge on a ranged attack, the target must also choose: drop prone, move to cover, or lose their Reaction.

Mobility Talents

    Zero-G Training: Ignore penalties in low gravity or zero gravity.

    Escape Artist: Gain Advantage to break restraints, cuffs, or grapples.

    Parkour: Climbing and vaulting do not cost extra movement.

    Jet Discipline: Gain Advantage when using jump packs, grav rigs, or aerial harnesses.

    Combat Roll: Once per round, after taking ranged damage, move a short distance to cover if any is available.

Social Talents

    Cool Under Fire: Gain Advantage on Discipline against fear, interrogation, or intimidation.

    Silver Tongue: Once per scene, reroll a failed Charm or Deceive check.

    Underworld Contacts: At the start of each mission, ask the GM for one black-market lead, rumor, or safehouse contact.

    Authority Voice: Gain +2 Command when ordering low-tier NPCs, guards, or frightened crowds.

    Sympathetic Face: Gain Advantage when trying to win trust from civilians, refugees, or uncertain recruits.

Tech Talents

    Field Repair: Repair armor or a damaged weapon during combat as an Action.

    Fast Slice: Your first successful Hacking check each scene also gives one minor extra benefit chosen by the GM.

    Drone Link: Gain a small support drone if you do not already have one. If you already have a drone, improve its durability or range.

    Cyber Affinity: Increase your safe Cyber Load by 1.

    Tinkerer: During downtime, mod one weapon, gadget, or armor piece without needing outside help.

Force Talents

    Force Adept: Learn one extra Force power.

    Defensive Flow: Once per round after using a Force power, gain +2 Defense until the start of your next turn.

    Force Leap Mastery: Leap costs no Resolve once per scene.

    Dark Pull: When you defeat a target with a Force power, recover 1 Resolve but take 1 Strain.

    Centered Breath: Once per scene, remove 1 Strain as an Action.

    Quiet Presence: Gain Advantage on checks to hide or conceal your use of the Force.

Vehicles and Ships

Fast stat blocks for chases, dogfights, and boarding actions.

Core Ship Stats

Hull = durability, Shields = temporary protection, Defense = how hard it is to hit, Handling = Pilot bonus, Speed = 1 slow, 2 normal, 3 fast, 4 interceptor.

Ship Attacks

Roll 1d20 + Pilot + ship weapon bonus against the target ship's Defense. Damage removes Shields first, then Hull.

Common Ship Actions

attack, evade, boost speed, take cover in debris or terrain, lock target, board, reroute power, spool jump.

System Strain

When a ship is reduced to half Hull, it gains 1 System Strain. At one-quarter Hull, it gains another.

For each System Strain, choose or roll one effect:

    −1 Defense

    −1 weapon attacks

    engines damaged

    sensors failing

    comms cut

    life support compromised

    hyperdrive unstable

Reroute Power

Choose one benefit until next turn:

    +2 Defense

    +2 damage

    restore 1d6 Shields

    ignore one System Strain penalty for a round

Section 14

GM Guide

Scene pacing, balance guidance, campaign pressure, and downtime.

GM-facing

What the GM Does

The GM describes the world, plays NPCs, sets difficulties, tracks Threat, and decides consequences. A good GM in this game keeps things moving and treats action as cinematic but dangerous.

Setting Difficulties

    10 for something routine under pressure

    12 for competent opposition or a decent lock

    15 for skilled resistance or dangerous terrain

    18 for elite guards, military systems, or advanced maneuvers

    22 for legendary feats or impossible odds

Using Threat Well

Threat should complicate, not negate.

    the alarm triggers after the players succeed

    a patrol rounds the corner at the worst time

    the bounty target flees through a crowd

    the locked door opens, but the hall beyond is occupied

    the players get the data, but the trace is active

Bad uses: undoing success entirely, nullifying smart preparation every time, or punishing players for taking risks in a genre built on risks.

Encounter Building

    1 Standard enemy per PC = fair fight

    2 Minions = 1 Standard

    1 Elite = 2 Standards

    1 Boss = 3–4 Standards

A strong encounter often includes some cover, one time pressure or objective, one enemy with a special role, and one complication if the fight drags on.

Improvised NPC Formula

    Weak NPC: Health 6, Defense 11, Attack +3, Damage 1d6

    Trained NPC: Health 10, Defense 13, Attack +4, Damage 1d8

    Dangerous NPC: Health 16, Defense 14, Attack +5, Damage 1d8+2

    Elite NPC: Health 22, Defense 15, Attack +6, Damage 1d10+2, one Reaction or special rule

Add an identity such as sniper, duelist, commander, slicer, heavy gunner, Force user, assassin, riot trooper, or elite guard.

Heat and Reputation

Reputation: Track separately with major groups: −2 enemy, −1 hostile, 0 neutral, +1 friendly, +2 allied.

Heat: 0 unknown, 1 noticed, 2 watched, 3 hunted, 4 sector alert, 5 kill/capture priority.

Increase Heat when the crew acts publicly, leaves witnesses, destroys expensive property, attacks a major faction, or botches a clean escape. Reduce Heat when the crew changes routes, identities, or ships, lays low, frames others, bribes officials, or wins local protection.

Captures and Prisoners

When a target reaches 0 Health, the attacker may declare kill, incapacitate, or capture.

A target can only be captured cleanly if they are restrained, stunned, surprised, downed, or fully overpowered narratively. Bosses may require special cuffs, sedation, disabling cyberware, or multiple successful checks.

Downtime

Between missions, allow characters to repair gear, install cyberware, buy or trade equipment, lower Heat, gather intel, cultivate contacts, train a talent narratively, and upgrade a ship. Downtime is where rebellion cells grow and bounty networks react.

Section 15

Species and Character Identity

Identity-rich species design without turning species into a second class system.

Default Species Rule

Species are important for identity, appearance, culture, and roleplay, but should not overshadow Callings, Talents, gear, or the Force.

Each species usually grants

    one minor trait

    one flavor feature or movement/sense adjustment

    sometimes one complication, social hook, or environmental note

Species should not usually grant

    big Attribute bonuses

    extra full actions

    large damage boosts

    multiple stacked combat powers

    broad immunity to whole sections of the game

Building a Species

    What do they look or feel like in play?

    What are they slightly better at?

    What is one interesting limitation, social complication, or vulnerability?

    How do they shape roleplay without replacing the character's Calling?

Sample Species Frameworks
Baseline Human

Adaptable: Gain one extra trained skill of your choice at character creation.

Agile Lithe Species

Quick Balance: Gain Advantage on checks to keep footing, squeeze through tight spaces, or avoid being knocked prone.

Large Hardy Species

Massive Frame: Gain +2 on Athletics checks to shove, carry, break, or hold position. Drawback: standard civilian gear, seats, or disguises may be awkward or costly to adapt.

Keen-Sense Species

Sharp Senses: Gain +2 to one kind of Notice check chosen at creation, such as scent, low-light vision, vibration, or distant sound.

Amphibious or Vacuum-Adapted Species

Environmental Tolerance: Choose one environment your species handles unusually well, such as underwater, toxic air, high cold, thin atmosphere, or low gravity.

Long-Lived Mystic Species

Deep Memory: Gain Advantage on one kind of Lore check tied to history, ancient rituals, or relics.

Small Species

Unassuming: Gain Advantage to hide in crowds, slip into maintenance spaces, or conceal yourself behind partial cover. Drawback: heavy weapons, armor, mounts, and brute-force tasks may be harder to use.

Synthetic or Artificial Species

Constructed Mind: Gain Advantage against poison, disease, and some fear effects. Drawback: healing may require Engineering instead of Medicine, and anti-tech weapons may affect you.

Species and Attributes

By default, species do not change Attribute modifiers.

Light Adjustment Rule

A species may shift one Attribute up by +1 and one different Attribute down by −1.

No Adjustment Rule

Species provide only traits and narrative identity. This is the default recommendation.

Species and the Force

Species do not usually grant Force powers by default. A species may have stronger traditions around the Force, myths, taboos, or unusual ways of perceiving it, but actual Force use should still come from the Adept Calling, a rare talent, or special training allowed by the GM.

Species and Equipment

Species shape what gear looks like more than what gear does. Larger species may need custom armor, smaller species may conceal weapons more easily, nonhuman body shapes may change cyberware fitting, and synthetic characters may use different repair procedures.

Best Practice for Play: Treat species as a strong roleplay anchor, a source of one memorable mechanical trait, and a way to imply galactic variety. In this game, a Hunter is dangerous because they are a Hunter, an Operative is dangerous because they are an Operative, and a Force user is dangerous because they have training and Resolve.

Player Rules Summary

Roll 1d20 + Attribute + Skill. Beat the Difficulty or Defense. Succeed by 5+ or roll a natural 20 to gain Edge. Fail by 5+ or roll a natural 1 and the GM gains Threat.

On Your Turn

1 Action, 1 Move, 1 Reaction.

Common Actions

Attack, Dash, Aim, Hide, Assist, Use Item, Hack, Stabilize, Use Force, Ready.

Damage and Death

At 0 Health, you are Downed. Roll 1d20 each turn. 10+ hold on. 9 or less gain 1 Wound. At 3 Wounds, you die.

Edge and Threat

Edge boosts player momentum. Threat fuels complications and escalation.

Section 17

Final Guidance for Play

The tone and pacing the game wants at the table.

This game runs best when

    every mission has a goal, a complication, and a consequence

    the players are allowed to be competent and bold

    the GM uses Threat to escalate scenes, not block them

    Heat and Reputation matter between sessions

    the Force feels rare, dangerous, and emotional

    blaster fights include movement, cover, and changing objectives

Play Principle

If you are unsure how to rule something, choose the faster option that keeps the scene moving.

Remnants of the Far Galaxy should feel like a story about survivors and strivers living in the ruins of greater powers, where a single crew can still change the fate of worlds.
